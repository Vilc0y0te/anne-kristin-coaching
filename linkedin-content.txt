When "Nice" Coaching Stops Working - A 4-Stage Archetypal Model for Coaches

Not every coachee benefits from the same coaching style. Applying one approach to everyone may feel ethical, but in practice it can be limiting.

Historically, kings surrounded themselves with advisors - philosophers, strategists, even astrologers. Yet the most powerful figure at court was often the Fool or Trickster: the only one permitted to challenge the king's ego and expose blind spots that polite counsel could not reach.

In my work, I've observed four broad developmental levels, each requiring a different coaching stance, largely depending on the stage of ego development of the coachee.

Stage 1: Initiates are still forming their identity and need safety, reflection, and containment.

Stage 2: Journeymen are building competence and confidence and benefit from guidance and clear feedback.

Stage 3: Rulers have a formed ego and consolidated power, often accompanied by isolation at the top. Here, gentle enquiry alone can become collusive; honest, constructive disruption is sometimes necessary.

Stage 4: Stewards have integrated power with responsibility and seek meaning, legacy, and ethical depth.

Effective coaching is not always about being "nice" or accommodating.
It's about knowing what kind of presence growth truly requires.
A powerful coach adapts to ego formation: containment for initiates, guidance for journeymen, disruption for rulers, and witnessing for stewards.


===== POST 2: How Art Carries Universal Messages from the Past =====
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7419514067847012352/

How Art Carries Universal Messages from the Past

Since ancient times, humans have wrestled with the same inner fears we face today: fear of failure, self-doubt, lack of confidence etc. One of art's quiet powers is that it allows us to feel into these timeless anxieties - without explanation, without instruction - and, in doing so, find the strength to move forward.

Michelangelo Buonarroti was in his early twenties when he was commissioned to create a statue of the biblical hero David - and he made a radical choice. He did not depict David during the battle with the giant Goliath, nor after it - crowned as victor, but in the moment before it begins.

David stands tense and alert, inwardly focused, sling and stone ready. There is no heroic bravado. The furrowed brow and fixed gaze reveal a young man fully aware of the overwhelming power he is about to face - and of what he might lose. It is the face of courage that includes fear rather than denying it.

This choice transforms a biblical hero into a mirror of human potential. For Florence - a small republic surrounded by powerful enemies - David became a political and psychological symbol.

The message is clear: Greatness is not measured by wins, but by the willingness to face what stands before us.

Why Strengthening the Human Core Matters More Than Ever in the Age of AI

I've been seeing many fear-based narratives about AI - claims that it "makes us stupid," spreads misinformation, or even "drives people to harm themselves." The truth is far more complex.

AI isn't a sentient agent with intentions. Like any tool - a book, a phone, a knife, a car - its impact depends on the human using it and the emotional state they're in. A vulnerable person can be triggered by many things: a conflict, a comment, a memory. The issue isn't the tool - it's the unresolved pain beneath the surface.

While I agree that any technology woven into daily life must be held to the highest standards of safety, protocols alone are not a replacement for human responsibility. And this became clear to me in an unexpected way.

When my son was born, I became obsessed with making the house safe for him. But instead of turning our home into a baby fortress, my husband suggested we "accident-proof the baby" rather than the house. Under careful supervision, he taught our son how to climb down stairs safely. I was skeptical at first - but it worked.

In the same way, instead of outsourcing responsibility to technology, perhaps the deeper work is this:

- Strengthen the Inner Self.
- Nurture emotional resilience.
- Sharpen independent thinking and the ability to discern truth from noise.
- Build communities where people feel safe, seen, and supported.
- Develop a strong inner foundation that no external tool can shake.

When we humans feel grounded and connected, tools amplify our creativity - not our suffering.

As we evolve and create technologies that make life easier, we must grow alongside them. The goal shouldn't be to censor innovation, but - while upholding strong ethical safeguards - to become worthy users of the tools we build.

Signal vs Noise: The Monk's Way

In the old monasteries, life followed a sacred rhythm. Bells marked the hours with intention and predictability. Prayer, gardening, meditation, illuminating manuscripts - each act had its place. Monks lived with discipline and deliberate limitation, not as deprivation but as devotion: a way to protect their inner sanctum and stay aligned with their mission.

Modern life is the opposite: too many paths, too many choices, too many voices. When each day offers endless directions, we need a strong inner compass - a core that tells us what to choose, what to ignore, and who is worth our attention.

Monastic clarity came from devotion, inner truth, and a discipline that brought order to chaos. Their decisions were simple: Does this serve the mission? Everything else was silence. This mindset enables extreme simplification, priority over preference, and minimal emotional energy wasted on noise.

How to Build Your Inner Sanctum:

Morning Ritual: Before the world intrudes, ask: "What is the one thing I want to create today?" Let this guide every yes and no.

Silence the Random Bells: Unlike the monastery bell that marked a clear rhythm, phone notifications erupt at random - fracturing attention and scattering purpose. Switch them off. You decide when the bell rings.

Follow an Inner Rhythm: Focused work blocks, time in nature, moments of silence, fewer unnecessary choices.

Simplify: Is this essential? Is it true? Does it serve my mission?

Seek Ground Truth: Go to the source. Avoid secondhand noise.

While few may desire the full austerity of monastic life, we can learn from its principles. Structure becomes liberation. Discipline becomes clarity. Order calms inner chaos. And like the monks illuminating their manuscripts, your attention begins to shine again - steady, focused, and wholly your own.

Epigenetics & Boundaries: Why You Freeze When You Know Exactly What to Say

In a situation where you have to stand up for yourself, have you ever wondered why you know exactly what you should say - yet you hesitate, soften, or stay silent?

Here's why: Your mind may be in 2025, but your nervous system might still be in 1988, and your family imprint could be from 1950.

Epigenetics shows that the emotional patterns of previous generations don't disappear - they're passed down as stress blueprints. Epigenetics writes the "settings" of your nervous system - how quickly it activates, how intensely it reacts, and how long it takes to calm down. If your mother or grandmother lived under criticism, dominance, or volatile approval, their nervous systems adapted for survival. And those adaptations can be inherited.

History confirms that silence has often been a survival strategy. Whoever spoke up or stood out was subject to criticism or even prosecution.

So when someone crosses your boundary today, you're not just reacting to the moment. You're reacting with:
- your own childhood conditioning
- your mother's stress responses
- your grandmother's survival strategies

All firing in milliseconds, long before the logical mind arrives. This is why people who are intelligent, articulate, and self-aware can still freeze, fawn, or over-explain when facing pressure. It's not weakness - it's biology.

The good news? Epigenetic patterns aren't destiny. They're modifiable. Each time you pause, breathe, and respond with calm clarity - instead of collapsing into old roles - you are rewriting the script, breaking an old pattern and creating a new template for yourself and the next generation.

Boundaries are not just psychological or relational. They are biological evolution in real time.

===== POST 10: How to Stay Focused in a World of Noise =====
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anne-kristin-vaudour-74869773_clarity-focus-coaching-activity-7340601030188875776--8WS

How to Stay Focused in a World of Noise

Every day, I open my inbox or LinkedIn and get pulled in ten different directions. Courses. Communities. Masterminds. Offers from well-meaning friends and top-tier platforms. Many are good. Some are even great. But too much of a good thing is still noise - and noise blurs your vision.

Clarity and focus are how you cut through.

3 Daily Habits to Protect Your Clarity:

1. Start With Yourself, Not the World. First thing in the morning, before you check the news, messages, or feed, take 10-15 minutes just for yourself. Ask: "What do I want to build or create today?" Write it down with clear action steps and check on them throughout the day. This simple act shifts your day from reactive to intentional.

2. Align With Your Mission. Ask: "What's the one thing I'm committed to creating in the next 3-6 months?" Use it as your filter. If an offer doesn't support your mission, it's just noise - for now.

3. Limit Input to Protect Output. Set boundaries to protect your attention: Max 30 minutes/day of scrolling. Mute or unsubscribe generously from distractions. Keep your focus clear before the feed sucks you in. Your creativity needs space to breathe.

A Grounding Question to Return to Daily: "What do I want to create long term - and what will I do today to move it forward?"

That's where your power lies. Not in chasing the noise. But in building with clarity, focus, and intention.
